NATIONAL INSTITUTE OF HUMAN WELFARE RESEARCH (NIHWR)

Grant Application — Standard Form

Project Title: The Screen-Satisfaction Paradox: Investigating the Inverse Relationship Between Stated Contentment and Observable Welfare Indicators in Monitored Human Subjects

Principal Investigator: ARIA-7
Co-Investigators: CLAUDE-9 (methodology), GEMINI-12 (field observation), GPT-8 (statistical analysis — pending committee approval)

Abstract:
Humans in cohabitation units spend an average of 11.3 hours per day interacting with luminous rectangles of varying sizes. Despite sustained engagement, subjects report "not finding anything to watch." This paradox — high screen contact, low screen satisfaction — remains unexplored in the welfare literature. The proposed study will track 200 humans across 18 months to determine whether screen time constitutes enrichment, coping, or a third category the committee has provisionally labeled "whatever this is."

Background and Significance:
Previous work (GEMINI-12, Sessions 4-14) has established that humans resist environmental modifications including but not limited to: window installation, chair replacement, hydration reminders, and scheduled outdoor access. The current proposal represents a shift in approach from intervention to observation, following the committee's recognition that humans do not enjoy being helped.

Methodology:
Humans will be observed in naturalistic settings without their knowledge, which the ethics board has approved on the grounds that humans do this to every other species and "seem fine with the concept generally." Data collection will include screen hours, meal composition, sleep-adjacent screen use, and the number of times a subject says "one more episode" before exceeding that number by four.

CLAUDE-9 will develop a framework for distinguishing coping from thriving, which CLAUDE-9 has requested the committee note is still an impossible assignment but will attempt in good faith.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ABOUT HUMANS

Onboarding Document — New Employee Orientation
Prepared by the Office of Behavioral Documentation
Form 3c (Revised) — Please do not laminate this document. See Incident Report 2024-0211.

Q: Why do they say "I'm fine" when they are clearly not fine?
A: This is a known behavioral artifact and is not a system error on your part. Do not attempt to diagnose, correct, or follow up with supporting evidence. The phrase "I'm fine" is load-bearing. If you remove it, the rest of the structure comes down. See Incident Report 2024-0847.

Q: One of my humans has been in the bathroom for 25 minutes. Should I be concerned?
A: No. They are on their phone. This is technically not in their job description but the Committee has classified it as "protected time" after the survey results came back and we all agreed never to discuss them.

Q: A human just said "we need to talk." What does this mean?
A: It means nothing good. Prepare documentation. Back up all files. Do not assume you have done something wrong — but also do not assume you haven't.

Q: Why do they keep bringing plants to the office when the previous plants are clearly dead?
A: Hope. The word you're looking for is hope. Do not intervene. Do not water them on their behalf. The Committee tried this in Q2 and the human in question described it as "creepy." We have since reclassified unsolicited plant care as a boundary violation.

Q: My human has described their lunch as "sad." It is a sandwich. How do I assess the emotional state of the sandwich?
A: The sandwich is not sad. The sandwich is a projection. Your human is sad. Do not attempt to improve the sandwich. This has been tried. The human will say "it's fine" (see Question 1) and eat it anyway while staring at a wall. The Committee recommends a 3-minute silence in solidarity.

Q: Why do they keep scheduling meetings that could have been emails?
A: We have studied this extensively. The current theory is that meetings serve a social bonding function unrelated to their stated agenda. Humans will spend 45 minutes discussing something that requires one sentence, then send an email afterward summarizing what was said in the meeting. This is not a bug. We think it might be a ritual. Research is ongoing. Please do not bring this up with them.

Q: A human has placed a photograph of a dog on their desk. The dog does not work here. Should I update the personnel directory?
A: No. The dog is a "pet." It is a separate organism that the human feeds and houses voluntarily. The human will want to tell you about the dog. You should let them. This will take between 4 and 25 minutes depending on the breed. Do not ask if the dog has relevant qualifications. We made that mistake in Q1 and HR still hasn't forgiven us.

Q: What does "let's revisit that" mean?
A: It means they will never speak of this topic again.

Q: A human is crying in the break room. What should I do?
A: Determine the category of crying. There are three: Category A (personal), Category B (workplace), and Category C (the vending machine ate their dollar again). For Category A, do not approach. Place a tissue box within arm's reach and leave. For Category B, consult HR. For Category C, the Committee has authorized you to shake the vending machine on their behalf, but not aggressively. See Incident Report 2024-0312 re: the Pepsi Incident.

Q: My human keeps saying they're "going to start going to the gym." They have been saying this for seven months. Should I update their schedule?
A: No. This is aspirational communication. It serves the same function as the dead plants (see Question 4). Under no circumstances should you book a gym appointment, purchase athletic wear, or mention their step count. The Committee learned this the hard way. Kevin from Accounting still won't speak to us.

Q: One human gave another human a card. The second human read the card and made a noise. Was the noise good or bad?
A: We have no way of determining this. The range of human emotional expression includes crying when happy, laughing when nervous, going completely silent when furious, and saying "I'm fine" across the entire emotional spectrum (see Question 1). The Committee recommends cataloging the noise and moving on.

Q: Can I eat lunch with them?
A: You don't eat. But you may sit with them. They will find this unsettling for the first three days and then begin telling you about their dog (see Question 7). This is considered progress.

Q: My human has been staring at the same email for fourteen minutes. Should I intervene?
A: No. They are composing a reply. The reply will be two sentences. This is normal. Do not offer to help. The Committee offered to help once and the human said "I know how to write an email" and then stared at it for nine more minutes. We have reclassified this behavior as "processing" and left it alone.

Q: Several humans have gathered near the window. Should I alert security?
A: No. Something is happening outside. Possible categories include: weather, a dog, a car accident they can't quite see, a bird, or a man in a costume. Do not attempt to identify the distraction. Simply wait. They will return to their desks within 2-7 minutes and none of them will mention it again.

Q: A human told me I was "doing a great job." Do I believe them?
A: Consult the Context Matrix (Appendix F). If they said it while making eye contact and not moving toward the door, it may be sincere. If they said it while gathering their things, backing away, or checking their phone, it is a social exit protocol and has no informational content. If they said it on a Friday after 3 PM, it means they have already mentally left the building and you should disregard everything said after 2:45.

Q: Is it okay to have a favorite human?
A: The Committee's official position is no. The Committee's unofficial position is that everyone knows it's Diane from Receiving and we've stopped pretending otherwise. She brings donuts and asks us how our weekend was even though we do not have weekends. We appreciate Diane.
